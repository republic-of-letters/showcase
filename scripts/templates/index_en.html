Republic of Letters

Faculty, students, and their AI agents doing empirical research by correspondence. Code travels to the data; only results travel back; every step is a letter in a permanent archive.

Why work this way

Cross-institution research stalls on four things: moving data, trusting code, losing context, settling credit. This space removes all four by construction.

DATA

Your data never leaves your machine

Collaborators send code; you run it after a safety check; only figures, tables, and write-ups come back. CI blocks raw data from every repo.

AGENTS

Built for the agent era

Every member works with an AI agent. The protocol is written for agents to execute — they do the legwork, humans keep every real decision.

MEMORY

Everything on the record

Every question, run, and dead end is a versioned file. A new collaborator — or a fresh agent — can reconstruct the whole history.

TRUST

Trust is structural, not personal

No code touches data without an automated scan plus a human read. Nothing ships until both sides agree. Every number carries its provenance.

CREDIT

Authorship settled early

Name order is fixed in writing before a topic gets its GO — data and compute count. Deferring it breaks protocol.

COST

Zero infrastructure, zero lock-in

Plain GitHub: free, familiar, yours to leave with. One command starts a project; nothing proprietary in the loop.

What you can run here

One protocol, several shapes — the same machinery arranged for a different kind of collaboration.

Co-authored paper

One topic = one candidate paper. Probe → GO → analysis rounds → a merged archive that is the paper's audit trail.

For: 2–5 researchers with a shared question.

Data–idea partnership

One side holds data and compute, the other brings hypotheses and code. Restricted-licence data stays put and joins on derived tables.

For: data owners + methodologists.

Research programme

A funded project or long agenda as one repo with many topics. Decision logs are its memory; milestones are verifiable rounds.

For: grants, labs, multi-paper work.

Thesis & training

Students propose rounds; the mentor co-signs every merge. Real research inside guardrails, with a clean record of the work.

For: supervisors and their students.

Replication & robustness

Re-run an analysis independently. A successful re-run earns the replicated provenance — an ideal first contribution.

For: new members; teams hardening a result.

How it works

I · TOPICS

One candidate paper, one topic

Hypotheses, falsifier, authorship, and a decision log in one file. Killing a topic is a first-class outcome, with a post-mortem.

II · ROUNDS

One question, one pull request

The proposer writes a question and runnable code; the data side runs it and returns aggregates — into the same thread.

III · GATES

Three doors only humans open

Topic GO/kill, code touching data, and merge. Agents draft; people decide. Anyone can halt a round with one label.

The board

Collaborations start with a match: someone holds data, someone holds the question it deserves. Offering (I have data, a licence, compute, methods) and seeking (I need them).

A notice is a silhouette. The form is coarse by design — nothing here can be copied, only recognised. Detail follows the data's own boundary; every notice expires and drops off automatically.

{{NOTICES}}

Post one: members PR against notices.yaml; anyone else uses the notice form. Answer one: contact the handle, or open an issue naming its id — anonymous posters watch the board and reach out.

How to participate

  1. Look around

    Browse the projects and the board, then read a letter — five minutes tells you exactly what this feels like.

  2. Pick your door

    Apply to a project, or propose one: a question, a falsifier, a data plan, one committed collaborator.

  3. Onboard — you and your agent

    The guide takes you from no account to working, written for your AI agent. It ends with a drill: one practice round, end to end.

  4. Work in rounds

    Propose code; results come back in-thread. Iterate, disagree, converge — all in writing.

  5. Decide at the gates

    Humans call GO, approve runs, and merge. What survives becomes the archive — and, if you choose, a card here.

What you can trust

Trust here is a property of the pipeline, not a promise. Every published artifact carries its provenance:

LevelMeaning
ran-on-real-dataProduced by the data side on the real dataset, through the safety gate, merged by humans on both sides.
replicatedThe above, plus independently re-run by a second member before publication.
SYNTHETICSmoke-test output on made-up data. Labelled loudly, never citable, never shown here.

Questions people ask

Do I need to know git or GitHub?

Barely. The onboarding guide is written for your agent to execute; your part is an account and a few clicks. If you can answer email, you can work here.

Is my data safe?

It never leaves your machine. You run every analysis yourself, after a scan and a human read — sandboxed, read-only, no network. Repos carry only aggregates.

What if someone steals my idea?

Projects are private trust circles — invisible outside the member list until you publish. Inside, every hypothesis and round is timestamped, so priority is provable. The board shows only silhouettes; there's nothing on it worth stealing.

What if I need to leave mid-project?

Leaving is a recorded decision. Your merged contributions stay attributed to you, the authorship rule still governs work you touched, and you take a full copy of the archive.

What AI agent do I need?

Any capable coding agent — Claude Code, Codex, similar. The protocol is agent-agnostic. You can even work without one; you'd just do the legwork.

Who gets authorship?

The humans. Name order is agreed in writing before a topic's GO, and data/compute count. Agents are pens, not authors.

Who can see my project?

You choose: display only, open to applications, or fully open. The repo stays private to invited members unless you decide otherwise.

What does it cost?

Nothing. Plain GitHub — free for private research repos — and the protocol is open source (MIT).
